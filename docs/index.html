Abstract

Achieving human-like perception and reasoning in Vision-Language Models (VLMs) remains a central yet elusive goal in artificial intelligence. This paper investigates a fundamental question: ***Can vision-language models truly see and understand the world as humans do?*** By introducing four diagnostic tasks, we reveal a critical limitation in current paradigms, demonstrating that state-of-the-art VLMs (including *Gemini 2.5 Pro*, *Claude 4*, and *O1*) exhibit **catastrophic failures** on some visual scenes that humans find trivial to comprehend. We propose three primary hypotheses to explain this performance gap: (1) a **data distribution mismatch**, where models lack sufficient exposure to the given tasks and fine-tuning may resolve this issue; (2) **architectural constraints** inherent in image encoders, which must simultaneously perform perception and reasoning tasks, potentially requiring alignment at both pixel and semantic levels; and (3) **fundamental limitations** in existing training paradigms that may inadequately capture human-like visual understanding. We present preliminary analyses of these factors and will provide deeper explanations and potential solutions in the next version. The evaluation dataset can be found in [HiddenTextImages](https://huggingface.co/datasets/HongchengGao/HiddenTextImages).

Four Diagnostic Tasks

We create four specialized datasets to evaluate the perception edge of VLMs, each designed to probe specific aspects of visual understanding that humans excel at but current models struggle with:

üåà ColorBlind

Similar to Ishihara tests, but augmented with confounding colored dots that are chromatically similar to central characters to increase difficulty. 150 test images

üéØ 3DCaptcha

üìñ ChineseLigatures

Intricate complex glyphs synthesized through character decomposition, morphological transformation, and fusion of multiple Chinese characters into different words or phrases.

Comprehensive Model Evaluation

We conduct extensive experiments on **15 models** across different architectures and types

üåê API-based Closed-source Models

üîì Open-source Models

Key Findings

Our comprehensive evaluation reveals a striking pattern: **all VLMs fail consistently across all four diagnostic tasks**. This universal failure across different model architectures, scales, and training paradigms suggests fundamental limitations in current approaches to visual understanding.

Key Insight

Despite remarkable performance on standard benchmarks, state-of-the-art VLMs including Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 4, and OpenAI o1 exhibit catastrophic failures on visual tasks that humans find trivial to solve.

Three Hypotheses

To explain this performance gap, we propose three primary hypotheses:

1. Data-driven Hypothesis

The pretraining data of those models lack domain-specific knowledge required for our proposed tasks. Under this hypothesis, continued training on the same-domain data could enable the models to acquire relevant knowledge and achieve improved performance.

2. Training Methodology Hypothesis

The training paradigm of those models is insufficient for learning our proposed tasks. Under this hypothesis, adopting different training approaches could lead to varying model performance outcomes.

3. Architectural Limitation Hypothesis

The architecture of those models inherently prevents them from acquiring the perceptual patterns necessary for our proposed tasks. Under this hypothesis, neither modifying training data nor altering training methodologies would enhance model performance on the corresponding task.

Evaluation Framework

We employ **Pass@1** and **Pass@4** metrics to assess model performance: - **Pass@1**: Measures the proportion of tasks correctly solved with a single generated solution - **Pass@4**: Evaluates the proportion of tasks correctly solved within four attempts These metrics provide comprehensive evaluation of both precision and resilience of model performance.
